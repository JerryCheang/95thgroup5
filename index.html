GreenNova - Smart Hub

A future-ready learning block that combines renewable energy, circular resource systems, AI building control, and student-centered spaces to turn sustainability into everyday campus life.

Members: Jerry, Kei Kei, Issac, Ken, Owen

Green Smart Hub by Group 5 on Sketchfab

Executive Summary

To celebrate the school's 95th anniversary, this project proposes a new Block D called the Green Smart Hub, designed as a sustainable and innovative learning space. The building addresses common problems in traditional campus buildings such as high energy consumption, resource waste, and weak links to the curriculum.

Based on the principles of Resource Circulation and Data-Driven Management, the design includes solar panels, rainwater collection, aquaponics, and smart energy systems to create a self-sustaining environment.

Through real-time data monitoring and hands-on experiments, students can connect knowledge from Physics, Chemistry, and Biology to real-world sustainability practices, turning the building into a living laboratory for interdisciplinary learning.

Design Concept & SDG Integration

The design concept for the new Block D is "Symbiosis and Circulation: A Breathing Educational Hub." We have moved beyond the traditional view of a building as merely a container for space, instead treating it as an organic, living entity. The building integrates active technologies (e.g., solar panels) with passive design (e.g., natural ventilation) to achieve self-sustaining cycles of energy and resources.

Furthermore, every functional zone within the building is an educational module. The sports hall's energy data is a case study for Physics, the aquaponics system is a joint laboratory for Biology and Chemistry, and the core AI data center is an application platform for Information Technology and Data Science. This "space-as-curriculum" approach ensures the building itself becomes the most dynamic teaching tool.

Sustainable Development Goals Integration

Our design aligns closely with the following eight United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs):

  • SDG 3: Good Health and Well-being

    Healthy spaces with natural ventilation, greenery, and fitness facilities.

    Read details

    Block D creates a healthy learning and working environment through natural ventilation, greenery, and wellness facilities. An indoor sports hall and fitness area on the first floor encourage physical activity, supporting mental and physical well-being. The rooftop garden and open social areas also help reduce stress and promote social interaction among students and teachers.

  • SDG 4: Quality Education

    A living classroom for hands-on STEM, AI, and environmental learning.

    Read details

    The new Block D serves as a living classroom for sustainability and innovation. The AI & Science Laboratory Floor and Smart Library provide spaces for hands-on STEM, AI, and environmental studies. Through these elements, Block D supports project-based learning and cultivates awareness of global sustainability.

  • SDG 6: Clean Water and Sanitation

    Rainwater is filtered and reused for aquaponics, toilets, and irrigation.

    Read details

    Rainwater collected on the roof is filtered and reused in a closed water cycle system. It supplies the aquaponics lab and non-potable water points such as toilets and irrigation. This reduces dependence on municipal water and demonstrates the efficient use of natural resources. The design educates students about water conservation and wastewater management, connecting science learning with responsible daily habits.

  • SDG 7: Affordable and Clean Energy

    Solar panels and battery storage support cleaner and smarter energy use.

    Read details

    A solar-panel array with sun-tracking capability on the rooftop generates renewable electricity to power lighting, ventilation, and devices across the building. Energy produced during the day is stored in batteries located in the basement and redistributed at night, allowing partial self-sufficiency. By replacing conventional power with solar energy, the system helps the school cut electricity consumption and reduce carbon emissions.

  • SDG 9: Industry, Innovation and Infrastructure

    AI and IoT systems automate comfort control and improve efficiency.

    Read details

    Block D integrates AI-based building management systems and IoT sensors that monitor temperature, humidity, and light intensity. These technologies automatically adjust fans, blinds, and LED lighting to maintain comfort and save energy. The project demonstrates how smart infrastructure and innovation can coexist with sustainability, making the school a model for future eco-technological campuses in Macau.

  • SDG 11: Sustainable Cities and Communities

    Shared spaces support community use, culture, and green social areas.

    Read details

    Community Integration & Resource Sharing: The multi-purpose auditorium/concert hall on the fourth floor, featuring professional acoustic design and LED displays, serves not only school functions and performances but is also available for community rental during holidays. This promotes cultural exchange and resource sharing between the school and the community, strengthening the school's role as a sustainable community cultural center. Green Public Spaces: The rooftop solar garden functions not only as a power generator but also as a green recreational and social space for students and staff, enhancing environmental livability and well-being.

  • SDG 12: Responsible Consumption and Production

    Water-food-waste cycles and smart energy control reduce resource waste.

    Read details

    Resource Circulation & Waste Reduction: We have established a complete micro-cycle for "water-nutrients-food." Rainwater collected on the roof is used for the aquaponics system on the second floor, reducing reliance on municipal water. The vegetables produced can be used in the school canteen, exemplifying local production and consumption. Meanwhile, the waste processing center in B1 manages the sorting, recycling, and decomposition of the building's waste, promoting waste reduction and resource recovery at the source. Energy Efficiency: Through solar power generation and an AI-powered energy management system that intelligently dispatches electricity (for example, prioritizing battery storage at night and dimming lights in unused sports halls), we drastically improve energy efficiency and demonstrate responsible energy consumption.

  • SDG 13: Climate Action

    Low-carbon design and AI energy optimization help reduce emissions.

    Read details

    Its combination of solar energy, green roofs, and natural lighting minimizes greenhouse-gas emissions and lowers dependence on fossil fuels. The AI-powered energy management system automatically dims lights in unused areas and adjusts power flow to prioritize efficiency. Through these efforts, the building contributes to local climate mitigation and raises awareness of carbon reduction in everyday school life.

Climate Data - Macau Environmental Context

According to the Macau Meteorological and Geophysical Bureau (SMG, 2025), Macau experiences an average annual rainfall of about 2,000 to 2,200 millimetres and around 1,900 to 2,000 hours of sunshine each year. This climatic pattern provides ideal conditions for sustainable resource management.

The high rainfall supports the use of a rainwater collection and filtration system, allowing recycled water to be used for irrigation, aquaponics, and toilet flushing. Meanwhile, the city's long sunshine duration ensures stable and efficient solar power generation throughout the year. Together, these two factors make Macau's environment highly suitable for the proposed Solar Garden and Rainwater Recycling System on the rooftop of Block D, enabling the building to achieve greater energy independence and water conservation.

Solid Waste and Recycling in Macau

According to the Macao Environmental Protection Bureau (DSPA, 2024), Macau's waste generation continues to rise while recycling performance has slightly declined, revealing a growing environmental challenge. In 2024, the city produced about 526,979 tonnes of municipal solid waste, equivalent to 2.1 kilograms per person per day. The total number of waste vehicles increased sharply from 9,984 in 2023 to 12,196 in 2024, a rise of 22.2 percent; light vehicles and heavy motorcycles accounted for the largest share of this growth.

At the same time, Macau's recycling rate fell from 22.0 percent in 2023 to 21.7 percent in 2024, continuing a gradual downward trend since 2021. Paper, electronic and electrical equipment, glass, and food waste remain the main categories of recovered materials, but the overall rate still lags behind regional sustainability targets. The DSPA has responded by expanding the community recycling network to eight "Eco Fun Stations," 45 recycling points, and 67 smart machines that accept paper, plastics, metals, glass, e-waste, and food waste.

Students' Daily Physical Exercise Time in Macau

Survey data shows that Macau secondary school students generally lack adequate daily physical activity, posing potential risks to their physical health. Fewer than 20% meet the recommended one hour of exercise per day, while the majority engage in insufficient activity - approximately 30% exercise only 10-30 minutes, and another 30% less than 10 minutes. This pattern indicates a widespread deficiency in physical exercise among students. Therefore, incorporating an indoor sports hall and gym into the new Block D is essential to provide accessible, consistent opportunities for physical activity and to support healthier developmental outcomes.
